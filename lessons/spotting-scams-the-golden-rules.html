Spotting scams: the golden rules

Who this is for: anyone who uses a phone or the internet. Time: about 15 minutes. You need: nothing — just read.

Scams are not a sign that you are careless. They are crimes, run by professionals, aimed at everyone — including young people and computer experts. The good news: almost every scam, however new it looks, follows the same old pattern. Learn the pattern once and you will recognize it for the rest of your life.

The three warning signs

A scam (a trick to take your money or personal information) almost always has these three ingredients:

  1. Urgency. "Act NOW." "Your account closes today." "Your grandson is in trouble this minute." Scammers rush you because calm people check.
  2. Secrecy. "Don't tell your family." "Don't tell the bank why you are withdrawing." Honest organizations never ask you to keep them secret.
  3. Money or personal details. It always arrives at the same place: pay something, buy gift cards, transfer "to a safe account", or "confirm" your password, PIN, or card number.

Urgency + secrecy + money = stop. You do not need to work out whether it is really a scam. The pattern itself is the reason to stop.

The golden rules

  1. It is always OK to hang up, delete, or ignore. You owe a stranger nothing — not politeness, not an answer, not a reason. A real organization will not be offended.
  2. Verify independently. If "your bank" calls, hang up. Then call the bank yourself using the number on the back of your card or on a statement — never a number the caller gave you, and never by pressing "call back". The same goes for "your grandchild": hang up and call the family number you already have.
  3. No honest caller ever needs your PIN or password. Not the bank, not the police, not the tax office, not a technician. Anyone who asks is a scammer. Every time. No exceptions.
  4. Gift cards and wire transfers are cash in an envelope. No real bill, fine, or fee is ever paid with gift cards. Anyone who asks for them is a scammer.
  5. Slow is safe. Real problems survive a cup of tea and a phone call to someone you trust. Fake problems fall apart.

The common shapes (same pattern, different costume)

  • The family emergency: "Grandma, it's me, I'm in trouble, please send money, don't tell Mum." Often a voice that sounds almost right — voices can now be imitated by computers. Hang up; call the family number you already have.
  • The fake bank or police call: "Fraud on your account — move your money to a safe account." Banks never do this. Hang up; call the bank's real number.
  • The prize: "You have won — just pay a small fee to release it." Real prizes never charge a fee.
  • The computer warning: a pop-up or caller says your computer is infected and offers to fix it remotely. Never let a stranger control your computer. Close the window or hang up.
  • The lonely heart: an online friend grows affectionate quickly, then has a sudden crisis that only money can solve. The affection is the tool; the crisis is the trap.

If it already happened

Being scammed is nothing to be ashamed of. It happens to millions of careful people every year, and speed matters far more than embarrassment.

  1. Tell someone you trust straight away.
  2. Call your bank's real number and tell them what happened — they can often stop or reverse payments if you are quick.
  3. Change any password you gave away, starting with your email.
  4. Report it to your country's fraud-reporting service — your bank can tell you which one.

Try it yourself

Cover the answers and decide: scam or not?

  1. A text: "Your parcel is held. Pay $1.99 within 2 hours: tap here."
  2. Your bank's app shows a normal monthly statement notification.
  3. A caller says he is from "computer support" and your machine is sending errors; he just needs remote access for ten minutes.

Answers: 1 — scam (urgency + small payment; delete it). 2 — normal (no urgency, no request). 3 — scam (never give a stranger access).

One-page summary (print me)

  • Urgency + secrecy + money = STOP.
  • Hanging up is always allowed.
  • Verify with a number you already have — never one they give you.
  • No one honest ever needs your PIN or password.
  • Gift cards = scam. "Safe account" = scam. Remote access = scam.
  • Scammed? No shame. Act fast: trusted person → bank → passwords → report.
