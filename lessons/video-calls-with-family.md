# Video calls with your family

**Who this is for:** you can tap and go home (see "Getting to know your
smartphone"), and someone you love lives too far away.
**Time:** 20 minutes plus one practice call. **You need:** your phone and
a family member willing to help — they will be delighted to be asked.

A **video call** (a phone call where you also see each other) is the
closest thing to being in the same room. Grandchildren grow up between
visits; a weekly video call means you watch it happen.

## The parts, in plain words

- The **camera** is a tiny eye at the top of your phone. There is one on
  the front (pointing at you) and one on the back.
- The **microphone** hears you. You do not need to lean into it or shout —
  talk as if the person were beside you.
- **Mute** is a button that switches your microphone off and on. Muted =
  they cannot hear you (useful when coughing; famous for being left on by
  accident — if they cannot hear you, look for the mute button).
- Video call apps differ in looks but all work the same way: you choose a
  person, tap a camera symbol, and they answer — like an ordinary phone
  call, plus faces.

## Receiving a call (start here — it is the easy side)

1. Ask a family member to video-call you at an agreed time.
2. When the phone rings, the screen shows who is calling and an **answer**
   button (usually green). Tap it once.
3. Say hello — you are done. Look at the screen, talk normally.
4. To finish, tap the red button. If the buttons have disappeared, tap
   the middle of the screen once and they come back.

## Making a call

1. Open the app your family uses for video calls (ask them which one —
   then it is the same app every time).
2. Find the person: tap the search symbol (a magnifying glass) and type
   their name, or scroll your recent conversations — the person you call
   often will be near the top.
3. Tap their name, then tap the **camera symbol** near the top of the
   screen. It rings; they answer; faces appear.

## Comfortable calling

- Prop the phone against something (books, a shelf) rather than holding
  it — steadier picture, no tired arm.
- Light in front of you (face a window), not behind you.
- If you see yourself instead of them — or the picture shows the ceiling —
  you are on the wrong camera. Look for the symbol of a camera with
  circling arrows and tap it to switch.
- If the picture freezes, nobody did anything wrong. Say "you froze",
  wait a moment, and if it stays stuck: hang up and call again. This is
  normal and everyone does it.

## Try it yourself

1. Receive one video call from a family member.
2. Hang up. Now you call them back — search, tap, camera symbol.
3. During the call: find mute, switch it on, count to three, switch it
   off. Now you have used the most confusing button on purpose.
4. Agree on a regular time. A standing weekly call is the whole point.

## One-page summary (print me)

- Green = answer. Red = hang up. Buttons hiding? Tap the middle of the
  screen.
- Muted = they cannot hear you. Camera-with-arrows = switch cameras.
- Prop the phone up; face a window.
- Frozen picture is normal: hang up, call again, laugh about it.
